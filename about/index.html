About Me

Welcome! My name is Tyler Margison, but you can call me kolorahl, or kolo, or dude; anything is fine. I want to thank you for visiting my website. A couple quick things before I tell you about myself:

  1. At the very top of every page is the main navigation bar. Use it to quickly jump between the main pages of this blog site.
  2. On the right side of every page is a "personal bar", which reminds you what my real name is, just in case you took me up on that offer to call me anything you wanted and forgot what the real one is. See? I got you covered; no awkward moments where you're standing around thinking, "What was his name again?" Oh, it also contains links to my GitHub and Twitter accounts as of writing this. This could expand to other online profiles I may have in the future that I wish to share.

Alright, we've got my name and the general setup of the website out of the way, what does that leave... Well, for starters, I am a software engineer. You could view it as a fancy term for "programmer," but I like to think that I take the role a little bit further: I design and implement software solutions for businesses. Historically that business has been in the video gaming industry, but the practices I try to hold myself to can be applied in any industry. And as for the rest about me, let's do this up list-style, because who doesn't like lists? Am I right?

  • I'm a gamer. I love board games, video games, and dice games. Some card games are fun too, like Munchkins and Cards Against Humanity. I also love pen & paper games, like Dungeons and Dragons and Shadowrun.
  • I watch anime. Yeah, it's stereotypical, whatever. I'm white, I'm a programmer, and I love video games; let's go par for the course. Favorite series: tie between Darker than Black and Code Geass.
  • I like learning new tech. Currently I'm working on a pet project involving Erlang, Riak, Websockets, and HTML 5. I've dabbled in Erlang and the HTML 5 Canvas before, but this will be my first major dive into them, and also the first time using Websockets and Riak. Exciting!
  • I use "large", easy-to-read, sans-serif fonts for all my personal websites. I also prefer dark backgrounds with light foreground coloring. Aside from being easier to work with as a developer, this man describes it quite nicely on his site FAQ. I'll post my favorite snippet below:
    I've chosen a black background for most of my text because it's easier on the eyes than staring at a white screen. Think about it: your monitor is not a piece of paper, no matter how hard you try to make it one. Staring at a white background while you read is like staring at a light bulb.
    - Maddox
